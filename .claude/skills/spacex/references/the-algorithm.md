# The Algorithm - Origins and Details

From Walter Isaacson's *Elon Musk* biography (2023).

## Origin

The algorithm was "shaped by the lessons [Musk] learned during the production hell surges at the Nevada and Fremont factories" in the late 2010s.

At production meetings at Tesla or SpaceX:
> "There is a nontrivial chance that Musk will intone, like a mantra, what he calls 'the algorithm.' His executives sometimes move their lips and mouth the words, like they would chant the liturgy along with their priest."

Musk himself says:
> "I became a broken record on the algorithm. But I think it's helpful to say it to an annoying degree."

According to Tim Berry (former SpaceX employee who spent a decade leading Falcon 9/Heavy upper-stage production):
> "The Algorithm was drilled into our minds. It's a five-step process for improving the design, making it ultimately easier to manufacture and finding opportunities to optimize along the way."

## The Five Steps - Exact Quotes

### Step 1: Question Every Requirement

> "Each should come with the name of the person who made it. You should never accept that a requirement came from a department, such as from 'the legal department' or 'the safety department.' You need to know the name of the real person who made that requirement."

> "Requirements from smart people are the most dangerous, because people are less likely to question them. Always do so, even if the requirement came from me. Then make the requirements less dumb."

### Step 2: Delete Any Part or Process You Can

> "Try very hard to delete the part or process. If parts are not being added back into the design at least 10% of the time, [it means that] not enough parts are being deleted."

> "The bias tends to be very strongly toward 'let's add this part or process step in case we need it.' But you can make 'in case' arguments for so many things."

> "Delete any part or process you can. You may have to add them back later. In fact, if you do not end up adding back at least 10% of them, then you didn't delete enough."

### Step 3: Simplify and Optimize

> "Only when you have walked through steps one and two can you start simplifying and optimizing. This particular order of steps protects you from doing unnecessary work."

> "It's possibly the most common error of a smart engineer to optimize a thing that should not exist."

### Step 4: Accelerate Cycle Time

> "You're moving too slowly, go faster! But don't go faster until you've worked on the other three things first."

> "If you're digging your grave, don't dig it faster."

### Step 5: Automate

> "An important part of this is to remove in-process testing after the problems have been diagnosed; if a product is reaching the end of a production line with a high acceptance rate, there is no need for in-process testing."

> "The big mistake in Nevada and at Fremont was that I began by trying to automate every step. We should have waited until all the requirements had been questioned, parts and processes deleted, and the bugs were shaken out."

## The Critical Insight: Order Matters

Musk admits:
> "I have personally made the mistake of going backwards on all five steps multiple times. In making Tesla's Model 3, I literally automated, accelerated, simplified and then deleted."

The correct order prevents:
- Optimizing things that shouldn't exist
- Automating broken processes
- Speeding up work that should be eliminated

## The Idiot Index

Related concept Musk developed:
> "The ratio of the total cost of a component to the cost of its raw materials. Something with a high idiot index--say, a component that cost $1,000 when the aluminum that composed it cost only $100--was likely to have a design that was too complex or a manufacturing process that was too inefficient."

> "If the ratio is high, you're an idiot."

Rockets had an extremely high idiot index. Musk calculated the cost of carbon fiber, metal, fuel, and other materials. The finished product cost at least 50x more than the raw materials.

The idiot index guides Steps 2 and 3: identifying what to delete and simplify.

## Continuous Application

After Step 5:
> "You're never satisfied. You're constantly going back and finding opportunities to challenge your requirements, deleting more parts, simplifying, optimizing, going faster, and then finally, opportunities to automate, but only once you've really boiled down to the baseline process."
